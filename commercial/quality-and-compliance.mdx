## Health-inspection-ready paperwork

For restaurants, the sanitization log is built to survive an inspection. When the health inspector walks in, you hand them the log. No scrambling, no "we'll email it over." The record is there, dated, and tied to each visit.

Crews working food premises and salons are trained on **Ontario food-premises regulations** and **WHMIS** (Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System), so the chemicals on site are handled and labelled the way the rules require.

## Protocols by industry

Different spaces are held to different standards. Crews are trained to the protocol the space needs.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Restaurants and food premises">
    Trained on **Ontario food-premises regulations** and **WHMIS**. Every visit produces a **sanitization log** plus **per-visit photo proof**, so the paperwork is health-inspection-ready and you can hand it to the inspector on demand.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Medical and dental">
    Crews follow **PIDAC** infection-prevention practices (Provincial Infectious Diseases Advisory Committee) and **OSHA** bloodborne-pathogen protocols, with a **colour-coded mop and cloth system** so a cloth used in one zone never crosses into another. Every visit produces an auditable sanitization log.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Salons and spas">
    Trained on **WHMIS** for the products and chemicals on site, with proper handling of towels, linen, and chemical-bottle disposal.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## The colour-coded mop system

To stop cross-contamination, the crew uses a colour-coded mop and cloth system. A cloth from a restroom never touches a clean reception desk.

| Colour | Zone |
|---|---|
| **Red** | Restrooms |
| **Blue** | Exam rooms |
| **Green** | Reception |

This is standard practice in medical and dental accounts and is part of how the **PIDAC** and **OSHA** protocols are kept honest in the field.
